What is the function of dopamine?

Dopamine is essential, revolving around the feeling of seeking out pleasurable situations, and is the primary neural basis for emotional mood, motivation, and attention. Motivation, for example, is caused by a surge of dopamine in the brain. Dopamine also affects sleep, learning, concentration, sensory processing, and body movements.

Dopamine is released when rewards are received, and the brain will also start to release dopamine while even thinking or working on goals in anticipation of receiving a reward. An example is children; if a child is told that when they finish their task, they will receive a piece of candy, dopamine will be released in anticipation of a reward and further increase motivation to finish the task.

LOW LEVELS

Dopamine can be too concentrated in specific parts of the brain, resulting in increased aggression, competitive nature, or problems in regard to impulse control.

POSSIBLE CONDITIONS:
Schizophrenia, hallucinations, binge eating, addiction to gambling

HIGH LEVELS

Lack of dopamine in specific areas of the brain can cause problems in muscle movement including cramps, spasms, or stiffness.


POSSIBLE CONDITIONS:
parkinson's disease, trouble sleeping, addiction to stimulant drugs, and ADHD

Synaptic Signaling of Dopamine

Dopamine(DA) is a neurotransmitter that is used to send messages between nerve cells that travel through neural-pathways in the brain. Dopamine has three major pathways,the mesocortical nigrostriatal, and mesolimbic pathways(picture above).

Dopamine is transported through the axon terminal into the synaptic cleft by synaptic vesicles. Dopamine then binds to the receptor site of either D1-D5 dopamine receptors(functions to the right) to carry out different functions(paracrine signaling).





Dopamine is also removed from the sympathetic cleft(area between two neurons) by a transporter protein called the dopamine transporter(DAT)that translocates the transmitter from the extracellular space back into the presynaptic neuron to respond to the next signaling(autocrine signaling).


To increase dopamine in parts of the brain,a dopamine reuptake inhibitor will block the dopamine transporter to increase concentrations of dopamine.

Dopamine receptors

D2: locomotion, attention, sleep, memory, learning

D3: cognition, impulse control, attention, sleep

D4: cognition, impulse control, attention, sleep

D5: decision making, cognition, attention, renin secretion

DOPAMINE AND ADHD

People with attention deficit hyperactive disorder, or ADHD, have at least one defective dopamine receptor(DR) gene(usually DRD2), making it more difficult for receptors to respond to neurons. This means that completing a short-term goal regarding a long-term goal, for example, does not release as much dopamine in anticipation as a neurotypical person receives, leading to a lesser boost in motivation.

Individuals with ADHD though have an increased sensitivity when rewards are reached. This is why people with ADHD partake in things that result in more immediate forms of dopamine, such as eating food, buying things, playing video games, and taking drugs. People with ADHD experience dopamine reuptake more often, resulting in high increases and decreases in motivation, concluding why individuals with ADHD have difficulty with long-term goals.
